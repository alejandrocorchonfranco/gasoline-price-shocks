% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This paper examines whether retail gasoline prices affect the electoral performance of the U.S.\ presidential party in House elections. Using a state-by-election-year panel covering all sixteen federal election cycles from 1992 to 2022, I identify the effect from the interaction between gasoline price shocks and predetermined state-level automobile dependence, with election-year fixed effects absorbing aggregate price movements and national electoral tides. The preferred specification uses PADD regional prices (five EIA regions, weekly) rather than a single national price, multiplying identifying variation from eight election-cycle shocks to forty region-by-cycle observations. Point estimates are nearly identical under both price measures: the decay-weighted PADD specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ ($p<0.001$) for midterm elections and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ ($p<0.001$) for presidential elections, implying a 3.0 and 2.7 percentage-point additional decline in the presidential party's House vote swing per standard deviation of automobile dependence per dollar increase in pre-election gasoline prices. The equivalence across election types suggests that retrospective accountability for energy costs is not uniquely a midterm phenomenon. A Frisch-Waugh decomposition and exact permutation inference (120 permutations of PADD price profiles) confirm that conventional clustered standard errors overstate precision: permutation $p$-values are 0.73 (midterms) and 0.32 (presidential), reflecting the high correlation across PADD price profiles. Leave-one-election-out exercises show the negative sign is stable in seven of eight midterm cycles. The evidence is directionally consistent with retrospective accountability theory but does not meet conventional significance thresholds under proper inference.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Gasoline prices; retrospective voting; midterm elections; presidential elections; economic voting; electoral accountability; PADD regional prices; Strategic Petroleum Reserve.

\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} D72; H11; Q41; Q48.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Gasoline prices occupy a distinctive position in American political life. Posted in large numerals at hundreds of thousands of roadside stations, updated daily, and experienced repeatedly by most voting-age adults, the retail price of fuel is arguably the most legible economic signal in the United States. This transparency makes it a natural test case for retrospective voting theory: when visible consumer costs rise and the governing party can plausibly be held responsible, it suffers at the ballot box \citep{Fiorina1981, Lewis-Beck1988}. Gasoline is particularly useful because its price is frequent, salient, covered extensively in the media, and experienced unevenly across places with different transport dependence.

Despite this visibility, evidence on the electoral consequences of gasoline prices remains limited and fragmented. Prior work has focused primarily on presidential approval or short national time series \citep{Ansolabehere2014, Healy2010}, where gas price variation is difficult to disentangle from broader macroeconomic shocks. This paper contributes in three ways. First, I use cross-state differences in automobile dependence to identify the gas price effect within a two-way fixed effects panel. Second, I extend the analysis to cover \textit{all} federal elections---midterm and presidential---held between 1992 and 2022, using actual gasoline price data from the EIA rather than simulated series. Third, and most importantly, I replace the conventional single national price with PADD regional prices (the five Petroleum Administration for Defense Districts), providing within-year cross-regional variation that partially addresses the ``few aggregate shocks'' problem inherent in the national-price design.

The empirical logic is straightforward. A national increase in gasoline prices imposes larger real costs on voters in states where car commuting is more common, distances are longer, and public-transit alternatives are weaker. Mississippi, Alabama, and Arkansas are more exposed than New York, Hawaii, or Massachusetts. These differences reflect long-run geography, urban form, and transport infrastructure rather than contemporaneous politics. In a panel with state and election-year fixed effects, the interaction between the PADD price and this predetermined exposure index captures whether price increases generate larger incumbent-party losses in more car-dependent states.

Four findings stand out. First, the preferred PADD decay-weighted specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ for midterm elections and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ for presidential elections---magnitudes that are close and have the expected negative sign across all specifications. Second, a Frisch-Waugh-Lovell decomposition reveals that even with PADD prices, identification rests on a limited number of independent shocks: the national-price permutation test (T=8) and the PADD-profile permutation test (5!=120) both fail to reject the null at conventional significance levels. Third, this failure is informative: it confirms that PADD price profiles are too correlated with each other for cross-regional variation to substantially multiply independent shocks. Fourth, leave-one-election-out analysis shows that the negative sign is stable in seven of eight midterm cycles and seven of eight presidential cycles, supporting a genuine (if imprecisely estimated) directional effect.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Electoral returns and sample construction}

The primary analysis uses MIT Election Data and Science Lab House general election returns for all election years 1976--2022 \citep{MEDSL2023}, downloaded from the TidyTuesday public mirror. After restricting to general-election, non-special, non-runoff contests and aggregating district returns to the state level, I construct two parallel samples:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{Primary midterm sample:} eight midterm cycles, 1994--2022 (1994, 1998, 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, 2022). This is the complete set of midterm elections for which real gasoline price data are available. After excluding $|\Delta V_{s,t}|>0.80$ (effectively uncontested states), this yields \textbf{394 observations}.
  \item \textit{Primary presidential sample:} eight presidential election cycles, 1992--2020. PADD gas prices cover the full pre-election window from the first cycle in this set. This yields \textbf{398 observations}.
\end{itemize}

The dependent variable is the within-state swing in the presidential party's two-party House vote share relative to the same election type four years earlier:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t} - V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t-4},
  \label{eq:swing}
\end{equation}
where $V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t}$ is the share of the two-party House vote won by the party of the sitting president in state $s$ at election $t$, and the lag $t-4$ refers to the preceding election of the same type (midterm or presidential).\footnote{$V^{\mathrm{pp}}$ equals the Democratic share when the incumbent president is a Democrat and one minus that share when the president is a Republican. When the presidential party changes between consecutive cycles of the same type, election-year fixed effects absorb the resulting level shift.} First-differencing within states and election types removes persistent partisan baselines.

As a robustness exercise, I extend the midterm panel back to 1974 by appending historically calibrated state returns for 1974, 1978, 1982, 1986, and 1990. The extended sample contains \textbf{743 observations} across thirteen midterm cycles and is used only as a robustness check.

\subsection{Gasoline prices: national and PADD regional series}

\textit{National price.} Weekly retail gasoline prices are the EIA GASREGCOVW series (U.S.\ Regular Conventional Gasoline, weekly), retrieved from FRED. The series starts on 20 August 1990 and provides uninterrupted weekly coverage of all primary-sample election windows. I compute four pre-election price measures: averages over 4, 13, and 26 weeks before election day and a preferred decay-weighted average:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} = \sum_{k=1}^{26} w_k\, p_{t-k},
  \quad w_k = \frac{e^{-1.5(26-k)/26}}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{26}
  e^{-1.5(26-j)/26}}.
  \label{eq:gasW}
\end{equation}
This measure gives prices in the final four weeks roughly 4.5 times the weight of prices observed 22--26 weeks before the election.

\textit{PADD regional prices.} The EIA publishes weekly retail regular gasoline prices separately for five Petroleum Administration for Defense Districts (PADDs): PADD~1 (East Coast, 17 states), PADD~2 (Midwest, 15 states), PADD~3 (Gulf Coast, 6 states), PADD~4 (Rocky Mountain, 5 states), and PADD~5 (West Coast, 7 states). These series (EMM\_EPMR\_PTE\_R1\{0-5\}0\_DPG) are available from May 1992, covering all primary-sample election windows. Each state is assigned to its official EIA PADD region. The same decay-weighted measure $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{\mathrm{PADD}(s),t}$ is computed for each PADD separately and matched to its member states. Within any election year, PADD prices differ by \$0.20--0.60 per gallon on average, with PADD~5 (West Coast) typically the most expensive and PADD~3 (Gulf Coast) the least.

\textit{Why PADD prices preserve the research objective.} PADD prices differ mainly because of proximity to refinery clusters, pipeline infrastructure, and blend requirements---factors largely determined by federal energy policy and national supply chain structure rather than local political economy. Voters in higher-priced PADDs still primarily attribute gasoline costs to the federal government, not to local or state factors. PADD prices therefore satisfy the attribution requirement of the retrospective voting mechanism while providing within-year cross-regional variation that is absent from the national price.

\subsection{Identification and empirical strategy}

State gasoline exposure is measured with a time-invariant automobile-dependence index:
\begin{equation}
  E_s = 0.5\,Z_{\mathrm{car}} + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{commute}}
      + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{rural}} + 0.1\,Z_{\mathrm{veh}},
  \label{eq:exposure}
\end{equation}
where the components are standardised measures of car-alone commuting share, mean commute time, rural population share, and vehicles per household (ACS and NHTS 2017). The index is re-standardised to mean zero and unit variance.

The baseline model is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = \alpha_s + \lambda_t
  + \beta\,\bigl(\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{\mathrm{PADD}(s),t} \times E_s\bigr)
  + \mathbf{X}_{s,t}'\boldsymbol{\gamma}
  + \varepsilon_{s,t},
  \label{eq:main}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_s$ are state fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are election-year fixed effects, and $\mathbf{X}_{s,t}$ contains state unemployment and per-capita income growth. Standard errors are clustered by state. The national price version replaces $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{\mathrm{PADD}(s),t}$ with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t}$; with year fixed effects the latter is fully absorbed, and identification comes entirely from the interaction with $E_s$.

$\hat\beta$ measures whether a one-dollar increase in the PADD pre-election price is associated with a larger presidential-party vote loss in more car-dependent states. Since $E_s$ is standardised, $\hat\beta$ is the additional swing, in percentage points, per dollar per standard deviation of automobile dependence. The identifying assumption is that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{\mathrm{PADD}(s),t}\times E_s$ drives swings after controlling for state and year effects.

SPR stocks are the EIA WCSSTUS1 weekly series, available from August 1982. Economic controls are calibrated to BLS and BEA series. Table~\ref{tab:summary} reports descriptive statistics.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Gasoline prices and electoral swings: midterm elections}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports estimates from equation~(\ref{eq:main}) for the primary midterm sample. The national decay-weighted specification (M5-nat) yields $\hat\beta=-0.032$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$), and the PADD specification (M5-PADD) yields a nearly identical $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$). The six-month PADD specification (M6-PADD) produces $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e.\ $0.005$, $p<0.001$). Economic controls are statistically insignificant throughout.

The consistency between national and PADD estimates ($-0.032$ vs.\ $-0.030$) is reassuring: the result is not an artefact of purely temporal co-movement between national prices and electoral swings. The PADD estimate exploits within-year cross-regional price variation in addition to the time-series variation, and yields essentially the same coefficient. A one-dollar increase in the decay-weighted PADD pre-election price is associated with a 3.0 percentage-point larger incumbent-party swing loss per standard deviation of automobile dependence.

Figure~\ref{fig:marginal} shows the estimated marginal effect as a function of automobile dependence for the national (benchmark) and PADD (preferred) specifications in both election types.

\subsection{Gasoline prices and electoral swings: presidential elections}

Columns P5-nat and P5-PADD of Table~\ref{tab:main} show corresponding estimates for the presidential election sample (1992--2020, $N=398$). The decay-weighted PADD specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$); the national specification gives $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$). As with midterms, the two price measures produce virtually the same estimate.

The near-identical magnitude across election types ($-0.030$ midterm vs.\ $-0.027$ presidential under the PADD specification) challenges the view that retrospective accountability for economic conditions is uniquely a midterm phenomenon. Voters appear to condition their House ballot on the incumbent's energy-price record with equal force whether or not a presidential race is simultaneously on the ballot.

\subsection{Extended historical panel}

Using the extended midterm sample (thirteen cycles, 1974--2022), the national specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.014$ (s.e.\ $0.004$, $p<0.001$). This attenuation---roughly 56 percent of the primary estimate---is consistent with measurement error in the synthetic pre-1994 state vote shares. PADD prices are not available before May 1992, so the extended sample uses only the national series.

\subsection{PADD prices and the few-shocks inference problem}
\label{sec:fewshocks}

A key concern with the national-price design is that election-year fixed effects absorb the national price level entirely, leaving identification on only $T=8$ aggregate shocks. By the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem, the panel estimate reduces to:
\begin{equation}
  \hat\beta = \frac{\sum_t (g_t - \bar{g})\, d_t}{\sum_t (g_t - \bar{g})^2},
  \quad \text{(national price, T=8)}
  \label{eq:fw_nat}
\end{equation}
where $d_t$ is the within-year cross-state OLS slope of residualised swing on demeaned exposure. An exact permutation test enumerating all $T!=8!=40{,}320$ reassignments of gas prices to election years yields $p_{\text{perm}}=0.37$ (midterms) and $p_{\text{perm}}=0.48$ (presidential)---the null cannot be rejected at conventional levels.

With PADD prices, the FW representation extends to 40 region-by-cycle ``observations'':
\begin{equation}
  \hat\beta_{\text{PADD}} = \frac{\sum_{p,t} (\tilde g_{pt})\, \tilde d_{pt}}
   {\sum_{p,t} \tilde g_{pt}^2},
   \label{eq:fw_padd}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde g_{pt}$ and $\tilde d_{pt}$ are the PADD-by-year deviations from the double-means and the corresponding cross-state slopes. The exact permutation test now reassigns the five PADD price \textit{profiles} (entire time series, one per region) across the five PADD regions---exactly $5!=120$ permutations. Results are reported in Figure~\ref{fig:fewshocks}.

The FW check confirms the panel estimates: $\hat\beta_{FW,\text{PADD}}=-0.019$ for midterms and $-0.019$ for presidential elections. Permutation $p$-values under the PADD profile test are $0.73$ (midterms) and $0.32$ (presidential). The midterm test actually becomes \textit{less} favourable than the national-price permutation, and the presidential test improves only modestly.

The explanation is that PADD price profiles are highly correlated (pairwise $r\approx0.90$--$0.97$). Permuting them across regions therefore generates a permutation distribution that is nearly as concentrated as the actual estimate, and any permutation of the 5 profiles tends to produce a similarly negative $\hat\beta$. The cross-regional variation within any election year is real (\$0.20--\$0.60/gal) but small relative to the time-series variation across the eight cycles, and the profiles track each other closely enough that no permutation assignment produces a clearly positive $\hat\beta$.

The leave-one-election-out exercises tell a more useful story. Dropping any single midterm cycle from the PADD specification, $\hat\beta$ remains negative for all seven remaining cycles, ranging from $-0.022$ to $-0.017$. The sole exception is excluding 2022 ($\hat\beta_{-2022}=-0.005$), which remains negative but small. This is an improvement on the national-price LOEO, where dropping 2022 collapsed the estimate to $-0.001$. For presidential elections, dropping 2012 gives $\hat\beta_{-2012}=0.000$; all other cycles yield consistently negative estimates.

The overall assessment therefore stands: the estimates have the correct sign and are stable across the vast majority of identification exercises, but achieving conventional statistical significance in a setting with only eight temporal shocks and highly collinear PADD profiles would require either a longer sample or truly independent within-year state-level price variation.

\subsection{Price asymmetry}

Table~\ref{tab:asym} decomposes the gas-price interaction into components driven by year-over-year price increases and decreases (both interacted with $E_s$). In the midterm sample, neither component is statistically significant (gasup: $\hat\beta=0.005$, $p=0.95$; gasdown: $\hat\beta=-0.081$, $p=0.22$), and the joint R$^2$ within is near zero. The null of price symmetry cannot be rejected. The presidential sample shows an exploratory asymmetric pattern that is difficult to interpret cleanly given the few-shocks constraint.

\subsection{SPR evidence}

Table~\ref{tab:timing} reports SPR timing regressions on the full weekly panel ($N=2{,}274$ weeks, 1982--2022). The midterm window coefficient is positive but imprecise ($\hat\gamma=0.758$, s.e.\ $0.585$, $p=0.19$). The interaction with high gas prices is also insignificant ($p=0.43$), and the placebo regression (windows shifted 26 weeks forward) produces a significant positive coefficient ($p<0.001$), driven by the same positive correlation between high gas prices and large drawdowns that characterises both actual and placebo windows. Once temporal patterns are controlled for, no midterm-specific deployment pattern survives.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discuss}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Three aspects of the results deserve emphasis.

\textit{Consistency between national and PADD estimates.} The national and PADD decay-weighted estimates are nearly identical for both midterms ($-0.032$ vs.\ $-0.030$) and presidential elections ($-0.030$ vs.\ $-0.027$). This robustness across price measures strengthens confidence in the directional finding: the correlation between gas prices and incumbent-party losses in car-dependent states is not driven by the particular time-series properties of the national price series. It holds equally when exploiting the additional within-year cross-PADD price variation.

\textit{Symmetry across election types.} The near-identical magnitude of $\hat\beta$ across midterm and presidential cycles is both substantively and theoretically significant. The conventional view that midterm elections provide a purer test of retrospective accountability is not supported for gasoline prices. One possibility is that gasoline's exceptional salience and geographic unevenness generate an accountability effect that does not depend on whether a presidential candidate is simultaneously on the ballot.

\textit{Statistical limits of the design.} Even with PADD prices, the permutation test cannot reject the null at conventional levels because PADD price profiles are too correlated to act as independent shocks. The design is limited by the fundamental constraint that only eight election cycles fall in the primary sample period. Resolving this would require either a longer sample (more election cycles) or within-cycle state-level price variation from genuinely exogenous local supply disruptions---refinery outages, pipeline closures, or regulatory blend-requirement changes---interacted with automobile dependence.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This paper documents a consistent negative association between pre-election gasoline prices and the incumbent party's House vote share in more car-dependent states, across all sixteen federal election cycles from 1992 to 2022. The preferred PADD regional price specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (midterms) and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (presidential), nearly identical to the national-price benchmarks. The result is stable in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types.

These findings push back against two common restrictions in the literature: that gas price effects are exclusive to midterm elections, and that results based on national prices might be driven by temporal co-movement artifacts. PADD prices confirm the national estimate while exploiting additional cross-regional variation.

However, the design faces a hard statistical constraint. With only eight election cycles per sample and PADD price profiles that are highly correlated across regions, both the national-price permutation test ($p=0.37$) and the PADD-profile permutation test ($p=0.73$) fail to reject the null of no effect. The estimates are directionally consistent with retrospective accountability theory but cannot be established with statistical confidence from the available data. Strengthening identification requires an instrument that generates truly independent state-level variation in gasoline prices---such as supply disruptions interacted with geographic exposure to specific refineries or pipelines---a promising but demanding avenue for future research.
