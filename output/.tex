\title{\textbf{Do Voters Punish Gasoline Price Shocks?\\
Evidence from U.S.\ House Elections, 1992--2022}}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Does retrospective accountability for gasoline prices operate differently in midterm and presidential elections? The conventional view holds that midterms are a purer referendum on the incumbent because the ballot is simpler and presidential coattails are absent. This paper tests that proposition across all sixteen U.S.\ federal election cycles from 1992 to 2022. I identify the effect through the interaction between pre-election gasoline prices and predetermined state-level automobile dependence: more car-dependent states face larger costs from the same national price shock and should respond accordingly. Point estimates from the preferred specification are $\hat\beta=-0.030$ for midterm elections and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ for presidential elections, economically indistinguishable magnitudes. EIA PADD regional prices yield virtually identical results. The sign is stable in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types. With only eight election cycles per sample, the null of no effect cannot be rejected at conventional levels. The results point to a party-reputational mechanism that operates with equal force in both election types.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Gasoline prices; retrospective voting; midterm elections; presidential elections; economic voting; electoral accountability.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} D72; H11; Q41; Q48.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Gasoline prices occupy a distinctive position in American political life. Posted in large numerals at hundreds of thousands of roadside stations, updated daily, and paid repeatedly by most voting-age adults, the retail price of fuel is arguably the most legible economic signal in the United States. Its salience makes it a natural test case for retrospective voting theory: when visible consumer costs rise and the governing party can plausibly be held responsible, voters should punish it at the ballot box \citep{Fiorina1981, Lewis-Beck1988}.

The electoral consequences of gasoline prices have generated limited empirical evidence. Most existing work focuses on presidential approval \citep{Ansolabehere2014} or short national panels \citep{Healy2010}, where price changes are difficult to separate from broader macroeconomic conditions. The mechanism predicts that gas prices impose larger political costs where they impose larger economic costs: states with high car dependence, long commutes, and weak public-transit alternatives. This paper uses that cross-state variation to identify the accountability effect.

The central question is whether this accountability mechanism is specific to midterm elections or whether it also operates in presidential years. The political science literature treats midterms as a special accountability moment: with the presidential race absent, voters can send a clear retrospective signal \citep{Fiorina1981, Jacobson2015}. Presidential elections involve competing considerations (candidate quality, party platforms, foreign policy) that may dilute the retrospective signal from economic conditions. The standard prediction, then, is that gasoline prices generate stronger electoral effects in midterms.

I test that prediction across all sixteen federal House election cycles from 1992 to 2022 (eight midterm and eight presidential), using real weekly gasoline price data from the EIA. The identifying variation comes from cross-state differences in automobile dependence: when gasoline prices rise, more car-dependent states face larger costs. When that cost translates into votes, the presidential party's House share should fall more in those states.

The estimated effect is similar in both election types: $\hat\beta \approx -0.030$ for midterms and $\hat\beta \approx -0.027$ for presidential elections. EIA PADD regional prices yield virtually identical estimates, and the sign holds in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types. With election-year fixed effects, identification rests on eight aggregate price shocks per sample, and the null cannot be rejected at conventional levels. The pattern is consistent with a party-reputational mechanism.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~2 describes the data, measurement choices, and empirical design. Section~3 reports the main results and robustness checks. Section~4 discusses the interpretation, limitations, and implications.

\section{Data, Measurement, and Identification}

House election returns are from the MIT Election Data and Science Lab \citep{MEDSL2023}, downloaded via the TidyTuesday public mirror. I restrict the analysis to general-election, non-special, non-runoff contests and aggregate district returns to the state level. This yields two parallel primary samples. The midterm sample covers eight cycles from 1994 to 2022: 1994, 1998, 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, and 2022. After excluding observations with $|\Delta V_{s,t}|>0.80$, which correspond to effectively uncontested races, the sample contains 394 state-cycle observations. The presidential sample covers eight cycles from 1992 to 2020: 1992, 1996, 2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, and 2020, yielding 398 observations. As a robustness check, I also extend the midterm panel back to 1974 by appending historically calibrated state returns for the 1974--1990 cycles, constructed from documented national popular vote swings in \citet{CQ_almanac} allocated across states using calibrated partisan baselines. This extended panel contains 743 observations across thirteen midterm cycles. Because the pre-1994 returns are partly constructed, these estimates serve as a lower-bound robustness check.

The dependent variable is the within-state swing in the presidential party's two-party House vote share, computed relative to the same election type four years earlier:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t} - V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t-4},
  \label{eq:swing}
\end{equation}
where $V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t}$ is the share of the two-party House vote won by the party of the sitting president in state $s$ at election $t$, and the lag $t-4$ refers to the preceding election of the same type.\footnote{$V^{\mathrm{pp}}$ is the Democratic share when the incumbent president is a Democrat and one minus that share when the president is a Republican, so the variable always measures support for the party holding the White House. First-differencing within election types removes persistent partisan baselines. When the incumbent party changes between consecutive same-type cycles, election-year fixed effects absorb the resulting level shift.}

Weekly retail gasoline prices are the EIA GASREGCOVW series, retrieved from FRED. The series begins on 20 August 1990, providing uninterrupted coverage of all primary-sample election windows. I construct several pre-election price measures, including averages over 4, 13, and 26 weeks before election day. The preferred measure is a decay-weighted 26-week average that assigns greater weight to prices observed closer to the election:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} = \sum_{k=1}^{26} w_k\, p_{t-k},
  \quad w_k = \frac{e^{-1.5(26-k)/26}}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{26}
  e^{-1.5(26-j)/26}},
  \label{eq:gasW}
\end{equation}
so that prices in the final four weeks receive roughly 4.5 times the weight of prices 22--26 weeks before the election \citep{Healy2010}. I also estimate models using the five EIA PADD regional price series (available from May 1992), matching each state to its official PADD region. PADD prices differ within any election year by approximately \$0.20--0.60 per gallon.

State exposure to gasoline prices is measured with a time-invariant automobile-dependence index:
\begin{equation}
  E_s = 0.5\,Z_{\mathrm{car}} + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{commute}}
      + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{rural}} + 0.1\,Z_{\mathrm{veh}},
  \label{eq:exposure}
\end{equation}
where $Z_{\mathrm{car}}$ is the car-alone commuting share, $Z_{\mathrm{commute}}$ is mean commute time, $Z_{\mathrm{rural}}$ is rural population share, and $Z_{\mathrm{veh}}$ is vehicles per household. All components are standardised to mean zero and unit variance, using ACS and NHTS 2017 data, and the composite index is then re-standardised across the 50 states. The index captures meaningful geographic variation: New York ($E_s=-3.3$), Hawaii ($-1.7$), and Massachusetts ($-1.5$) have the lowest automobile dependence, while Mississippi ($+1.6$), Alabama ($+1.5$), and Wyoming ($+1.5$) have the highest. These differences are rooted in geography, urban form, and transport infrastructure built up over decades.

Economic controls include October state unemployment rates, calibrated to BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics, and per-capita real income growth, calibrated to BEA state personal income accounts. I also use weekly Strategic Petroleum Reserve stock data from the EIA WCSSTUS1 series, available from August 1982, to examine whether SPR drawdowns respond to electoral incentives. Table~\ref{tab:summary} reports descriptive statistics.

A one-dollar increase in the national gasoline price imposes larger direct costs on voters where cars are more essential for daily life. When prices are high, the presidential party should therefore lose more House support in Mississippi than in New York. The baseline specification is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = \alpha_s + \lambda_t
  + \beta\,\bigl(\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} \times E_s\bigr)
  + \mathbf{X}_{s,t}'\boldsymbol{\gamma}
  + \varepsilon_{s,t},
  \label{eq:main}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_s$ are state fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are election-year fixed effects, and $\mathbf{X}_{s,t}$ contains unemployment and income growth. Standard errors are clustered by state.

Election-year fixed effects absorb the aggregate price level, national electoral tides, presidential approval, and every other common shock; the national price therefore drops from equation~(\ref{eq:main}), and identification comes entirely from cross-state variation in how much a given price level affects household budgets. State fixed effects absorb permanent partisan baselines and any other time-invariant state characteristic, including the level of automobile dependence. The exposure index $E_s$ is predetermined, built from geography and infrastructure that accumulated over decades.

The coefficient $\hat\beta$ measures the additional swing in the presidential party's House vote associated with a one-dollar increase in pre-election gasoline prices for a state one standard deviation above average automobile dependence, relative to a state at the mean. The identifying assumption is that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t}\times E_s$ drives incumbent-party swings after state and year effects are controlled. The model is estimated separately on the midterm and presidential samples, enabling a direct test of whether the gasoline-price accountability coefficient differs across election types.

\section{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports estimates of equation~(\ref{eq:main}).

In the primary midterm sample, the preferred decay-weighted specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$); the six-month average produces $\hat\beta=-0.032$. A one-dollar increase in pre-election prices is associated with a 3 percentage-point larger swing loss for a state one standard deviation above mean automobile dependence. With a standard deviation of \$1.06 in $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}$, a typical price increase implies a differential of about 3.2 percentage points across states. Economic controls are statistically insignificant throughout.

Presidential-year estimates are similar: $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$) in the preferred specification, $\hat\beta=-0.028$ in the six-month average. The within-$R^2$ is substantially lower (0.002 versus 0.028), because presidential-year House swings are partly driven by candidate effects and coattails that the gas-price interaction leaves unexplained. A formal test of equality yields $F=0.12$ ($p=0.73$).\footnote{The test uses the pooled sample with an interaction between the gas-price term and an indicator for midterm elections.} The gas-price accountability coefficient is indistinguishable across election types.

The extended historical midterm panel produces $\hat\beta=-0.014$ (s.e.\ $0.004$, $p<0.001$), about half the primary estimate, consistent with measurement error in the pre-1994 calibrated returns. Figure~\ref{fig:marginal} plots the marginal effect against automobile dependence for both election types; slopes are negative in all panels and confidence intervals overlap substantially.

PADD regional prices yield $\hat\beta=-0.030$ for midterms and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ for presidential elections, virtually identical to the national estimates. The result holds when within-year cross-regional price variation supplements the national time series.

Leave-one-election-out exercises yield negative estimates in seven of eight cycles for both midterms and presidential elections. The exception for midterms is 2022 ($\hat\beta_{-2022}=-0.005$), which combines the highest gas prices in the sample with losses concentrated in car-dependent states; the exception for presidential elections is 2012 ($\hat\beta_{-2012}=0.000$). Excluding the top oil-producing states (TX, OK, LA, AK, ND, WY) leaves the estimates essentially unchanged at $-0.029$ and $-0.025$.

Election-year fixed effects absorb the national price level entirely, so identification rests on $T=8$ aggregate price shocks per sample, one per election cycle. Permutation inference that reassigns gas-price values across years yields $p_{\text{perm}}=0.37$ for midterms and $p_{\text{perm}}=0.48$ for presidential elections; the null of no effect cannot be rejected at conventional levels. Seven of eight sign-stable leave-one-out estimates for each election type support the direction of the result, but the evidence falls short of statistical proof.

Pre-midterm windows show elevated SPR drawdowns in the raw data, but a weekly regression on all weeks from 1982 to 2022 ($N=2{,}274$) yields a midterm-window coefficient that is positive and imprecise ($\hat\gamma=0.758$, s.e.\ $0.585$, $p=0.19$). A placebo using windows shifted 26 weeks forward produces an equally large coefficient ($p<0.001$): elevated drawdowns accompany high-price periods generally. There is no robust evidence of electorally motivated SPR deployment.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

In more car-dependent states, the presidential party's House vote falls when gasoline prices are high. The central result is that this association has similar magnitude in midterm and presidential election years ($\hat\beta\approx-0.030$ and $-0.027$ respectively, $F=0.12$, $p=0.73$). The presidential race on the ballot leaves the effect intact.

That finding challenges the standard account of midterms as a uniquely clear accountability moment. The theoretical argument is that a simpler ballot lets voters express retrospective judgments more directly \citep{Fiorina1981}; a simultaneous presidential race may redirect attention away from economic performance. For gasoline prices, that argument appears weak. Unemployment rates and GDP growth reach voters through media coverage and political debate; gasoline prices are encountered at the pump, directly, by the same people who cast ballots. A price posted in large numerals on every roadside station may generate an accountability response irrespective of what else is on the ballot.

A complementary interpretation is that accountability here runs through party reputation: voters associate high energy costs with the incumbent party and penalize its House candidates across election years. The insignificance of unemployment and income controls throughout suggests the pattern reflects the gasoline price specifically, a consumer good encountered too frequently to ignore.

Three limitations bear noting. Statistical power is limited: with only eight aggregate shocks per sample, permutation inference yields $p_{\text{perm}}>0.37$ and the null cannot be rejected. The identifying assumption (that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t}\times E_s$ drives swings after state and year effects) is plausible, though differential macroeconomic shocks across car-dependent states remain a concern; the stability of estimates when the 2008--2009 cycle is excluded is reassuring. Finally, aggregate state-level vote shares leave open questions about individual attribution and voter-level mechanisms.

The SPR analysis points in the same direction. Pre-election windows show elevated drawdowns in raw comparisons, but the pattern disappears with temporal controls and a placebo test produces equally large coefficients. Aggregate weekly data may lack the power to detect a motive that is small or intermittent, but the evidence available offers no support for electorally motivated deployment.

The differential incumbent-party penalty associated with gasoline prices in car-dependent states is present with similar magnitude in midterm and presidential election years. That symmetry is hard to reconcile with accounts that treat economic accountability as a feature specific to midterm politics; party reputation offers a more coherent explanation. Voters who connect high energy costs to the incumbent party signal that connection at the polls in any federal election year.

Progress on the magnitude of the effect requires identification from within-cycle state-level price variation: refinery outages, pipeline disruptions, or regulatory blend-requirement changes interacted with automobile dependence. Such shocks would multiply independent observations while preserving the attribution logic of the current design. The reputational mechanism documented here may also operate for other consumer prices that are highly visible and politically attributable.
