\title{\textbf{Do Voters Punish Gasoline Price Shocks?\\
Evidence from U.S.\ House Elections, 1992--2022}}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Does retrospective accountability for gasoline prices function differently for midterm and presidential elections? The traditional view is that midterm elections are a purer referendum on the incumbent since the ballot is simpler and presidential coattails are absent. In this paper we test that claim in all sixteen U.S. federal elections from 1992 to 2022. I am able to identify the effect of pre-election gasoline prices and predetermined state-level automobile dependence: more car-dependent states are more expensive for a national price shock and should respond. The point estimates from the preferred specification are $\hat\beta=-0.030$ for midterm elections and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ for presidential elections, and they are economically equivalent. EIA PADD regional prices are virtually identical. The sign is stable in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both types of elections. Since there are only eight cycles per sample, the null of no effect cannot be rejected at the conventional level. The results suggest that the party reputation mechanism is the same in both types of elections.

\bigskip \noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Gasoline prices; retrospective voting; midterm elections; presidential elections; economic voting; electoral responsibility.

\medskip \noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} D72; H11; Q41; Q48.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Gasoline prices are a significant part of American political life. Posted in large numerals at hundreds of thousands of roadside stations, updated daily, and paid repeatedly by most voting-age adults, the retail price of fuel is perhaps the most visible economic signal in the United States. It is also a perfect case study for retrospective voting theory: when consumer costs increase and the governing party can plausibly be held responsible, voters should punish it at the ballot box \citep{Fiorina1981, Lewis-Beck1988}.

The electoral consequences of gasoline prices have little to no empirical evidence. Most existing work focuses on presidential approval \citep{Ansolabehere2014} or short national panels \citep{Healy2010} in which price changes are difficult to separate from larger macroeconomic conditions. The mechanism predicts that gas prices impose more political costs where they impose more economic costs: states that have a high car dependence, long commutes, and weak public-transit alternatives. This paper uses that cross-state variation to identify the accountability effect.

The question is whether this accountability mechanism is specific to midterm elections or whether it also exists in presidential years. The political science literature treats midterms as a special accountability moment: absent the presidential race, voters can send a clear retrospective signal \citep{Fiorina1981, Jacobson2015}. Presidential elections are characterized by competing issues (quality of candidates, party platforms, foreign policy) that can dilute the retrospective signal derived from economic conditions. However, the general prediction is that gasoline prices raise the electoral impact in midterms.

I test that prediction across all sixteen federal House election cycles from 1992 to 2022 (eight midterm and eight presidential) through real weekly gasoline price data from the EIA. The difference is driven by state-by-state differences in automobile dependence: when gasoline prices increase, more car-dependent states have higher costs to pay. If that cost translates into votes, then the presidential party’s House share should fall more in those states.

The estimated effect is similar for both election types: for the midterms we find $\hat\beta \approx -0.030$ and for the presidential elections $\hat\beta \approx -0.027$. EIA PADD regional prices are almost identical and the sign holds in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types. Due to the fixed-year effects in the election-year prices, one could identify the effect by eight aggregate price shocks in each sample and one cannot reject the null at usual levels. The pattern is consistent with a party-reputational mechanism.


\section{Data}

House election returns from the MIT Election Data and Science Lab \citep{MEDSL2023}, downloaded from the public mirror TidyTuesday. I restrict the analysis to general-election, non-special, non-runoff contests and aggregate district returns to the state level. This yields two parallel primary samples. The midterm sample covers eight cycles from 1994 to 2022: 1994, 1998, 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, and 2022. After excluding observations with $|\Delta V_{s,t}|>0.80$, which correspond to effectively uncontested races, the sample contains 394 state-cycle observations. The presidential sample covers eight cycles from 1992 to 2020: 1992, 1996, 2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, and 2020, yielding 398 observations. As a robustness check, I also extend the midterm panel back to 1974 by appending historically calibrated state returns for the 1974--1990 cycles, constructed from documented national popular vote swings in \citet{CQ_almanac} allocated across states using calibrated partisan baselines. This extended panel contains 743 observations across thirteen midterm cycles. Because the pre-1994 returns are partly constructed, these estimates serve as a lower-bound robustness check.

The dependent variable is the within-state swing in the presidential party's two-party House vote share, computed relative to the same election type four years earlier:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t} - V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t-4},
  \label{eq:swing}
\end{equation}
where $V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t}$ is the share of the two-party House vote won by the party of the sitting president in state $s$ at election $t$, and the lag $t-4$ is the election prior to election $t$. $V^{\mathrm{pp}}$ is the Democratic share in a Democratic election and one minus that share for a Republican election, so the variable always takes into account support for the party currently holding the White House. First-differencing between different types of elections removes the basis for partisan biases.

Weekly retail gasoline prices are the EIA GASREGCOVW series retrieved from FRED. The series begins on August 20, 1990, and covers all primary samples of election. We construct a number of pre-election price measures including the average over 4, 13, and 26 weeks before election day. The most preferred price measure is a decay-weighted 26-week average with more weight for prices in the months before the election:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} = \sum_{k=1}^{26} w_k\, p_{t-k},
  \quad w_k = \frac{e^{-1.5(26-k)/26}}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{26}
  e^{-1.5(26-j)/26}},
  \label{eq:gasW}
\end{equation}
so prices in the final four weeks receive roughly 4.5 times the weight of prices 22 to 26 weeks before the election \citep{Healy2010}. I also estimate models using five EIA PADD regional price series (available from May 1992) and match each state to its official PADD region. PADD prices vary within any election year by about \$0.20 to \$0.60 per gallon.

State exposure to gasoline prices is measured with a time-invariant automobile-dependence index:
\begin{equation}
  E_s = 0.5\,Z_{\mathrm{car}} + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{commute}}
      + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{rural}} + 0.1\,Z_{\mathrm{veh}},
  \label{eq:exposure}
\end{equation}
where $Z_{\mathrm{car}}$ is the car-alone commuting share, $Z_{\mathrm{commute}}$ is the mean commute time, $Z_{\mathrm{rural}}$ is the rural population share, and $Z_{\mathrm{veh}}$ is vehicles per household. Each component is standardized to mean zero and unit variance according to ACS and NHTS 2017 data, and the index is then re-standardised across the 50 states. The index demonstrates meaningful geographic variation: New York ($E_s=-3.3$) is least dependent on cars, Hawaii is ($-1.7$), and Massachusetts is ($-1.5$), while Mississippi ($+1.6$), Alabama ($+1.5$), and Wyoming ($+1.5$) are the highest dependent. These differences are rooted in geography, urban form, and transport infrastructure built up over decades.

Economic controls include October state unemployment rates, which are calibrated to BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics, and per-capita real income growth, which is calibrated to BEA state personal income accounts. I use weekly Strategic Petroleum Reserve stock data from the EIA WCSSTUS1 series, available from August 1982, to determine whether SPR drawdowns are affected by electoral motivations. Table~\ref{tab:summary} shows descriptive statistics.

A one-dollar increase in the national gasoline price brings higher direct costs to voters in places where cars are more important for daily life. When prices are high, the presidential party should lose more House support in Mississippi than in New York. The baseline specification is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = \alpha_s + \lambda_t
  + \beta\,\bigl(\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} \times E_s\bigr)
  + \mathbf{X}_{s,t}'\boldsymbol{\gamma}
  + \varepsilon_{s,t},
  \label{eq:main}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_s$ are state fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are election year fixed effects, and $\mathbf{X}_{s,t}$ are unemployment and income growth. The standard errors are clustered by state.

Election-year fixed effects absorb the aggregate price level, national electoral tides, presidential approval, and all other common shocks. The national price therefore drops from equation~(\ref{eq:main}), and identification comes entirely from the cross-state variation in how much a given price level affects household budgets. State fixed effects also take into account permanent partisan baselines and any other time-invariant state characteristic, including the level of automobile dependence. The exposure index $E_s$ is predetermined, built from geography and infrastructure accumulated over decades.

The coefficient $\hat\beta$ measures the additional swing in the presidential party's House vote associated with a one-dollar increase in gasoline prices for a state one standard deviation above the average automobile dependence, relative to a state at the mean. The identifying assumption is that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t}\times E_s$ drives incumbent-party swings after state and year effects are controlled. We estimate the model separately for the midterm and presidential samples, enabling a direct test of whether the gasoline-price accountability coefficient differs across election types.

\section{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports estimates of equation~(\ref{eq:main}).

In the primary midterm sample, the preferred decay-weighted specification yields the following value of $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e. $0.006$, $p<0.001$) and the six-month average gives $\hat\beta=-0.032$. A \$1 increase in the prices at the pre-election time corresponds to a 3 percentage point larger swing loss for a state that is one standard deviation above the average dependence of cars. With a standard deviation of \$1.06 in $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}$, a typical price rise of about 3.2 percentage points is expected to be different for each state. In all cases, economic controls are negligible from above.

Presidential-year estimates are similar: $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e. $0.006$, $p<0.001$) in the preferred one and $\hat\beta=-0.028$ in the six month average. The within-$R^2$ is much lower (0.002 vs. 0.028) because House swings in presidential years are somewhat mediated by the candidate effects and the associated coattails that the gas price interaction leaves unanswered. A formal test of equality gives $F=0.12$ ($p=0.73$).

The long historical midterm panel yields $\hat\beta=-0.014$ (s.e. $0.004$, $p<0.001$), about half the primary estimate, which is consistent with the measurement error in the calibrated returns before 1994. Figure~\ref{fig:marginal} shows the marginal effect against automobile dependence for both types of elections. The slopes are negative in all the panels and the confidence intervals are close.

PADD regional prices give us $\hat\beta=-0.030$ for midterms and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ for presidential elections, almost identical to the national estimates. The analysis can be generalized to the scenario in which within-year cross-regional price variations are added to the national time series.

The leave-one-election-out exercises yield negative estimates in seven of eight cycles for both midterms and presidential elections. The exception for the midterms is 2022 ($\hat\beta_{-2022}=-0.005$) since it combines the highest gas prices in the sample with losses concentrated in car dependent states. The exception for presidential elections is 2012 ($\hat\beta_{-2012}=0.000$). Without the largest oil-producing states (TX, OK, LA, AK, ND, WY) the estimates remain essentially unchanged at $-0.029$ and $-0.025$.

We see that fixed effects during election years take over the national price level, so it is only possible to identify the effect in a single sample of different election years if we use $T=8$ of price shocks for each sample. If we permute the price level over years to assign the gas price over each election, we get $p_{\text{perm}}=0.37$ in the midterms and $p_{\text{perm}}=0.48$ in presidential elections and the null of no effect cannot be rejected at the standard levels. Seven of eight sign-stable leave-one-out estimates for each election type support the result, but we cannot show statistical support.

Pre-midterm windows show high SPR drawdowns in the raw data, but a weekly regression of all weeks from 1982 to 2022 ($N=2{,}274$ weeks) yields a midterm window coefficient that is positive and imprecise ($\hat\gamma=0.758$, s.e. $0.585$, $p=0.19$) and a placebo with windows shifted 26 weeks forward has the same strength of coefficient ($p<0.001$). Higher drawdowns are usually associated with high price periods. There is no clear evidence for electorally motivated SPR deployment.

\section{Discussion}

In more car-dependent states, the House vote of the presidential party falls when gasoline prices are high. The main result is that this association has similar magnitude in midterm and presidential election years ($\hat\beta \approx -0.030$ and $\hat\beta \approx -0.027$ respectively, $F=0.12$, $p=0.73$) and the presidential race on the ballot is still in effect.

This is against the belief of midterms as a very clear accountability moment. A simpler ballot allows voters to make retrospective judgments more clearly \citep{Fiorina1981}; it might also shift attention away from the economic performance of the economy in a simultaneous presidential race. For gasoline prices, that argument is weak. Unemployment rates and GDP growth reach voters through media coverage and political debate; gasoline prices are experienced at the pump directly by the same people who cast ballots. A price in large numbers on every roadside station may trigger an accountability response for the same reason: no matter what else is on the ballot.

A complementary interpretation is that accountability here runs through party reputation: voters associate high energy costs with the incumbent party and penalize its House candidates over election years. The scarcity of unemployment and income controls throughout suggests the pattern reflects the gasoline price specifically, a consumer good that too many of us deal with.

The results are not without their limitations. Only eight aggregate shocks per sample are considered and permutation would give $p_{\text{perm}} > 0.37$ and the null cannot be ruled out. The identification assumption (that there is no time-varying state shock with respect to the $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} \times E_s$ that makes swings due to the state and year) is reasonable, but there are still differential macroeconomic shocks in car-dependent states in the analysis. The stability of estimates when the 2008--2009 cycle is excluded is also reassuring. Finally, the share of votes among different states is not clear that all states are equally affected and the mechanisms behind the correlation of state and voter shares are not clear.

The SPR analysis is similar. Pre-election windows show large drawdowns in raw comparisons, but the pattern disappears with time controls, and placebo test yields equally large results. If weekly data are not enough to detect a motive that is small or intermittent, there is no evidence to support electorally motivated deployment.

The differential incumbent-party penalty associated with gasoline prices in car-dependent states is similar to that in midterm and presidential election years. That symmetry is hard to reconcile with accounts that regard economic accountability as an issue specific to midterm politics; party reputation is a more coherent explanation. Voters who link high energy costs to the incumbent party signal that connection at the polls in any federal election year.

Progress toward understanding the magnitude of the effect would be made by recognizing within-cycle state-level price variation: refinery outages, pipeline interruptions or regulatory blend-requirement changes interacting with automobile influence. Such shocks would double our observations while maintaining the attribution logic of our design. This reputational mechanism may also be present in other consumer prices that are heavily visible and politically sensitive.
