Evidence from U.S.\ House Elections, 1992--2022}}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Does retrospective accountability for gasoline prices operate differently in midterm and presidential elections? The conventional view holds that midterms are a purer referendum on the incumbent because the ballot is simpler and presidential coattails are absent. This paper tests that proposition using all sixteen U.S.\ federal election cycles from 1992 to 2022. I identify the effect through the interaction between pre-election gasoline prices and predetermined state-level automobile dependence: more car-dependent states should respond more strongly to the same national price shock. Point estimates from the preferred specification are $\hat\beta=-0.030$ for midterm elections and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ for presidential elections--economically indistinguishable magnitudes. Replacing the national price with EIA PADD regional prices yields virtually identical results. The sign is stable in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types. With only eight election cycles per sample, however, the evidence should be read as directionally consistent with retrospective accountability theory rather than as statistically definitive. The implication is that accountability for energy prices, if it exists, appears to operate through party reputation rather than through mechanisms specific to midterm elections.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Gasoline prices; retrospective voting; midterm elections; presidential elections; economic voting; electoral accountability.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} D72; H11; Q41; Q48.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Gasoline prices occupy a distinctive position in American political life. Posted in large numerals at hundreds of thousands of roadside stations, updated daily, and paid repeatedly by most voting-age adults, the retail price of fuel is arguably the most legible economic signal in the United States. Its salience makes it a natural test case for retrospective voting theory: when visible consumer costs rise and the governing party can plausibly be held responsible, voters should punish it at the ballot box \citep{Fiorina1981, Lewis-Beck1988}.

Despite this visibility, the electoral consequences of gasoline prices remain surprisingly understudied. Most existing work focuses on presidential approval ratings \citep{Ansolabehere2014} or uses short national panels \citep{Healy2010}, where gasoline price changes are difficult to separate from broader macroeconomic conditions. The theoretical mechanism predicts that gas prices should matter most where they matter most in daily life: states with high car dependence, long commutes, and weak public-transit alternatives. This paper exploits that geographic heterogeneity as a source of identification.

The central question is whether retrospective accountability for gasoline prices is specific to midterm elections or whether it also operates in presidential election years. The political science literature treats midterms as a special case of accountability politics. Because the presidential race is absent from midterm ballots, voters can deliver a clear signal about their satisfaction with the incumbent \citep{Fiorina1981, Jacobson2015}. Presidential elections, by contrast, involve more competing considerations--candidate quality, party platforms, foreign policy--that may dilute or displace the retrospective signal from economic conditions. On this view, gasoline prices should generate stronger electoral effects in midterms than in presidential years.

This prediction has not been directly tested. The present paper tests it using all sixteen federal House election cycles from 1992 to 2022--eight midterm and eight presidential--with real weekly gasoline price data from the EIA. I identify the effect through the interaction between pre-election gasoline prices and predetermined state-level automobile dependence. More car-dependent states should experience the same national price shock more intensely; if gasoline prices generate retrospective punishment, the presidential party's House vote should fall more in those states when prices are high.

The results do not support a midterm-specific accountability mechanism. Point estimates from the preferred specification are nearly identical across election types: $\hat\beta \approx -0.030$ for midterms and $\hat\beta \approx -0.027$ for presidential elections. Replacing national prices with EIA PADD regional prices yields virtually identical estimates, and the sign is stable in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types. The design is not powerful enough to deliver a definitive causal estimate: with election-year fixed effects, the relevant price variation comes from only eight aggregate shocks per sample. The evidence is therefore best read as directionally consistent with retrospective accountability theory, and especially with a party-reputational mechanism, rather than as statistically conclusive proof of voter punishment.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~2 describes the data, measurement choices, and empirical design. Section~3 reports the main results and robustness checks. Section~4 discusses the interpretation, limitations, and implications.

\section{Data, Measurement, and Identification}

House election returns are from the MIT Election Data and Science Lab \citep{MEDSL2023}, downloaded via the TidyTuesday public mirror. I restrict the analysis to general-election, non-special, non-runoff contests and aggregate district returns to the state level. This yields two parallel primary samples. The midterm sample covers eight cycles from 1994 to 2022: 1994, 1998, 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, and 2022. After excluding observations with $|\Delta V_{s,t}|>0.80$, which correspond to effectively uncontested races, the sample contains 394 state-cycle observations. The presidential sample covers eight cycles from 1992 to 2020: 1992, 1996, 2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, and 2020, yielding 398 observations. As a robustness check, I also extend the midterm panel back to 1974 by appending historically calibrated state returns for the 1974--1990 cycles, constructed from documented national popular vote swings in \citet{CQ_almanac} allocated across states using calibrated partisan baselines. This extended panel contains 743 observations across thirteen midterm cycles. Because the pre-1994 returns are partly constructed, these estimates serve as a lower-bound check rather than a primary result.

The dependent variable is the within-state swing in the presidential party's two-party House vote share, computed relative to the same election type four years earlier:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t} - V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t-4},
  \label{eq:swing}
\end{equation}
where $V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t}$ is the share of the two-party House vote won by the party of the sitting president in state $s$ at election $t$, and the lag $t-4$ refers to the preceding election of the same type.\footnote{$V^{\mathrm{pp}}$ is the Democratic share when the incumbent president is a Democrat and one minus that share when the president is a Republican, so the variable always measures support for the party holding the White House. First-differencing within election types removes persistent partisan baselines. When the incumbent party changes between consecutive same-type cycles, election-year fixed effects absorb the resulting level shift.}

Weekly retail gasoline prices are the EIA GASREGCOVW series, retrieved from FRED. The series begins on 20 August 1990, providing uninterrupted coverage of all primary-sample election windows. I construct several pre-election price measures, including averages over 4, 13, and 26 weeks before election day. The preferred measure is a decay-weighted 26-week average that assigns greater weight to prices observed closer to the election:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} = \sum_{k=1}^{26} w_k\, p_{t-k},
  \quad w_k = \frac{e^{-1.5(26-k)/26}}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{26}
  e^{-1.5(26-j)/26}},
  \label{eq:gasW}
\end{equation}
so that prices in the final four weeks receive roughly 4.5 times the weight of prices observed 22--26 weeks before the election. This specification is motivated by evidence that voters weight recent economic conditions more heavily \citep{Healy2010}. I also estimate models using the five EIA PADD regional price series, available from May 1992. Each state is assigned to its official PADD region. PADD prices differ within any election year by approximately \$0.20--0.60 per gallon, providing within-year cross-regional variation in addition to the time-series variation.

State exposure to gasoline prices is measured with a time-invariant automobile-dependence index:
\begin{equation}
  E_s = 0.5\,Z_{\mathrm{car}} + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{commute}}
      + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{rural}} + 0.1\,Z_{\mathrm{veh}},
  \label{eq:exposure}
\end{equation}
where $Z_{\mathrm{car}}$ is the car-alone commuting share, $Z_{\mathrm{commute}}$ is mean commute time, $Z_{\mathrm{rural}}$ is rural population share, and $Z_{\mathrm{veh}}$ is vehicles per household. All components are standardised to mean zero and unit variance, using ACS and NHTS 2017 data, and the composite index is then re-standardised across the 50 states. The index captures meaningful geographic variation: New York ($E_s=-3.3$), Hawaii ($-1.7$), and Massachusetts ($-1.5$) have the lowest automobile dependence, while Mississippi ($+1.6$), Alabama ($+1.5$), and Wyoming ($+1.5$) have the highest. These differences reflect long-run geography, urban form, and transport infrastructure rather than contemporaneous electoral politics.

Economic controls include October state unemployment rates, calibrated to BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics, and per-capita real income growth, calibrated to BEA state personal income accounts. I also use weekly Strategic Petroleum Reserve stock data from the EIA WCSSTUS1 series, available from August 1982, to examine whether SPR drawdowns respond to electoral incentives. Table~\ref{tab:summary} reports descriptive statistics.

The empirical design exploits cross-state heterogeneity in the consequences of a common national price shock. A one-dollar increase in the national price of gasoline imposes larger direct costs on voters in states where cars are more essential for daily life. If gasoline prices generate retrospective punishment, the presidential party should lose more support in states such as Mississippi than in states such as New York when gasoline prices are high. The baseline specification is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = \alpha_s + \lambda_t
  + \beta\,\bigl(\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} \times E_s\bigr)
  + \mathbf{X}_{s,t}'\boldsymbol{\gamma}
  + \varepsilon_{s,t},
  \label{eq:main}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_s$ are state fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are election-year fixed effects, and $\mathbf{X}_{s,t}$ contains unemployment and income growth. Standard errors are clustered by state.

The design has three important implications. First, election-year fixed effects absorb the aggregate price level, national electoral tides, presidential approval, and any other common shock. The national gasoline price therefore does not enter separately; identification comes from whether the same national price level is associated with different electoral swings in states with different exposure to gasoline costs. Second, state fixed effects absorb permanent differences across states in partisan alignment, economic structure, and the time-invariant level of automobile dependence. Third, the exposure index is predetermined: it reflects geography and infrastructure built up over decades, not election-year politics.

The coefficient $\hat\beta$ measures the additional swing in the presidential party's House vote associated with a one-dollar increase in pre-election gasoline prices for a state one standard deviation above average automobile dependence, relative to a state at the mean. The identifying assumption is that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t}\times E_s$ drives incumbent-party swings after state and year effects are controlled. The model is estimated separately on the midterm and presidential samples, enabling a direct test of whether the gasoline-price accountability coefficient differs across election types.

\section{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports estimates of equation~(\ref{eq:main}) for the midterm and presidential samples, alongside the extended historical midterm panel. The table is organised to compare election types and price measures directly.

In the primary midterm sample, the preferred decay-weighted national specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$). The six-month average produces an almost identical estimate, $\hat\beta=-0.032$. These estimates imply that a one-dollar increase in pre-election gasoline prices is associated with a 3.0 percentage-point larger decline in the presidential party's House vote swing for a state one standard deviation above mean automobile dependence, relative to a state at the mean. The standard deviation of $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}$ is \$1.06, so a one-standard-deviation price increase implies an additional differential response of about 3.2 percentage points across a one-standard-deviation contrast in exposure. Economic controls are statistically insignificant throughout, consistent with election-year fixed effects absorbing aggregate macroeconomic conditions.

The presidential-year estimates are almost the same. In the preferred specification, the coefficient is $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$), while the six-month average gives $\hat\beta=-0.028$. These estimates are economically indistinguishable from the midterm estimates. The within-$R^2$ is substantially lower in the presidential sample (0.002 versus 0.028 for midterms), reflecting the fact that in presidential election years the swing in House vote shares is largely driven by presidential candidate effects and coattails; these are absorbed by the election-year fixed effects but generate residual state-level variance that the gas-price interaction does not explain. A formal test of equality yields $F=0.12$ ($p=0.73$), so the data do not reject equal coefficients across election types.\footnote{The test uses the pooled sample with an interaction between the gas-price term and an indicator for midterm elections.} This similarity is the central empirical result: the evidence does not support the view that gasoline-price accountability is uniquely or substantially stronger in midterm elections.

The extended historical midterm panel produces a smaller estimate, $\hat\beta=-0.014$ (s.e.\ $0.004$, $p<0.001$), attenuated by approximately half relative to the primary estimate. This attenuation is consistent with classical measurement error in the historically calibrated pre-1994 returns. Figure~\ref{fig:marginal} plots the estimated marginal effect as a function of automobile dependence for the preferred midterm and presidential specifications. The slopes are negative in both panels and the confidence intervals overlap substantially, visually reinforcing the equality of effects across election types.

The result is not sensitive to replacing the national price series with PADD regional prices. Using region-specific gasoline prices yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e.\ $0.006$) for midterms and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e.\ $0.006$) for presidential elections, virtually identical to the national estimates. This consistency suggests that the result is not driven by the time-series properties of a single national price series; it survives when within-year cross-regional price variation provides additional identifying variation.

Leave-one-election-out exercises point in the same direction, although they also reveal the influence of specific cycles. For midterms, seven of eight leave-one-out estimates are negative, ranging from $-0.022$ to $-0.017$ in the PADD specification. The only near-zero estimate arises when 2022 is excluded ($\hat\beta_{-2022}=-0.005$), because that cycle combines the highest gas prices in the sample with incumbent-party losses concentrated in more car-dependent states. For presidential elections, seven of eight leave-one-out estimates are also negative; excluding 2012 produces a near-zero estimate ($\hat\beta_{-2012}=0.000$). Excluding the top oil-producing states--TX, OK, LA, AK, ND, and WY--leaves the estimates essentially unchanged: $\hat\beta=-0.029$ for midterms and $\hat\beta=-0.025$ for presidential elections.

These conventional standard errors should be interpreted cautiously. Election-year fixed effects absorb the national price level entirely, so the identifying variation comes from only $T=8$ aggregate price shocks per sample--one per election cycle--rather than from hundreds of independent state observations. Permutation inference that reassigns gas-price values across years yields $p_{\text{perm}}=0.37$ for midterms and $p_{\text{perm}}=0.48$ for presidential elections. The null of no effect cannot be formally rejected at conventional levels. The evidence is therefore directionally consistent and sign-stable across cycles, but it should be read as suggestive rather than conclusive.

Finally, I examine whether Strategic Petroleum Reserve drawdowns respond to electoral incentives by regressing weekly SPR releases on pre-midterm window indicators, gas prices, and month and year fixed effects, using all weeks from 1982 to 2022 ($N=2{,}274$). The midterm-window coefficient is positive but imprecise ($\hat\gamma=0.758$, s.e.\ $0.585$, $p=0.19$). More informatively, a placebo regression using windows shifted 26 weeks forward produces an equally large coefficient ($p<0.001$), indicating that large drawdowns coincide with high-price periods in general, not specifically with electoral windows. There is no robust evidence of electorally motivated SPR deployment.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

The central finding of this paper is that the gas-price accountability pattern is not confined to midterm elections. The presidential party performs worse in more car-dependent states when gasoline prices are high, but the magnitude of this association is almost the same in midterm and presidential election years. If gasoline prices affect House election outcomes, they appear to do so regardless of whether the presidential race is simultaneously on the ballot.

This equivalence challenges the standard account of midterms as a uniquely clear accountability moment. The usual theoretical argument is that midterms provide a simpler ballot: without a presidential race to distract from or redirect blame, voters can more cleanly express retrospective judgments about the incumbent party's performance \citep{Fiorina1981}. The evidence here is difficult to reconcile with that view for energy prices specifically. Gasoline may be unusually salient because it is visible, frequently paid, and personally experienced. Unlike unemployment rates or GDP growth, which require media intermediation to become politically relevant, gasoline prices are encountered directly at the pump. That visibility may saturate the accountability signal regardless of ballot complexity.

A more plausible interpretation is that the mechanism operates through party reputation rather than through midterm-specific strategic behavior. If voters associate high energy costs with the president's party, the reputational cost can spill over to House candidates in both midterm and presidential election years. This interpretation is consistent with the finding that unemployment and income growth controls are insignificant throughout: the estimated pattern does not appear to reflect general state economic conditions, but a specific and highly visible consumer price.

Several caveats apply. First, the design rests on only eight aggregate price shocks per sample. The estimates are stable in sign and similar in magnitude across election types, but permutation inference cannot reject the null. Second, the identifying assumption--that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t}\times E_s$ drives incumbent-party swings after state and year effects--is plausible but untestable. The most credible threat would be a national shock that affects car-dependent states differentially in ways correlated with both gasoline prices and electoral swings, such as a recession that hits manufacturing-heavy or rural states alongside an energy price spike. Excluding the 2008--2009 cycle leaves the main estimates essentially unchanged, but the concern cannot be eliminated entirely. Third, the design uses aggregate state-level House vote shares. It does not identify individual-level mechanisms or directly observe how voters attribute responsibility for energy prices.

The SPR evidence reinforces the need for caution. If governments actively used petroleum reserves to manage electoral exposure to high prices, we would expect larger releases before elections when prices are high. The data show some raw association, but no evidence that survives temporal controls. This is consistent with either no electoral motive or with a motive that is too small and intermittent to detect in aggregate weekly data.

This paper therefore does not provide definitive causal proof that gasoline prices determine House election outcomes. Instead, it shows that the differential incumbent-party penalty associated with gasoline prices in car-dependent states is similar in midterm and presidential election years. That pattern weakens the view that gasoline-price accountability is a midterm-specific phenomenon and points instead to a broader party-reputational mechanism.

Resolving the remaining ambiguity requires identification from within-cycle state-level price variation, for example from locally exogenous supply disruptions, refinery outages, or pipeline disruptions interacted with automobile dependence. Such shocks would generate more independent variation while preserving the attribution logic of the current design. Gasoline is unusually salient, but the same reputational mechanism may extend to other consumer prices that are visible, repeatedly experienced, and politically attributable.
