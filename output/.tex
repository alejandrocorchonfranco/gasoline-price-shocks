% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Does retrospective accountability for gasoline prices operate differently in midterm and presidential elections? The conventional view holds that midterms are a purer referendum on the incumbent because the ballot is simpler and presidential coattails are absent. This paper tests that proposition using all sixteen U.S.\ federal election cycles from 1992 to 2022. I identify the effect through the interaction between pre-election gasoline prices and predetermined state-level automobile dependence: more car-dependent states should respond more strongly to the same national price shock. Point estimates from the preferred specification are $\hat\beta=-0.030$ for midterm elections and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ for presidential elections---economically indistinguishable magnitudes. Replacing the national price with EIA PADD regional prices yields virtually identical results. The sign is stable in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types. With only eight election cycles per sample, however, the evidence should be read as directionally consistent with retrospective accountability theory rather than as statistically definitive. The implication is that accountability for energy prices, if it exists, appears to operate through party reputation rather than through mechanisms specific to midterm elections.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Gasoline prices; retrospective voting; midterm elections; presidential elections; economic voting; electoral accountability.

\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} D72; H11; Q41; Q48.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Gasoline prices occupy a distinctive position in American political life. Posted in large numerals at hundreds of thousands of roadside stations, updated daily, and paid repeatedly by most voting-age adults, the retail price of fuel is arguably the most legible economic signal in the United States. Its salience makes it a natural test case for retrospective voting theory: when visible consumer costs rise and the governing party can plausibly be held responsible, voters should punish it at the ballot box \citep{Fiorina1981, Lewis-Beck1988}.

Despite this visibility, the electoral consequences of gasoline prices remain surprisingly understudied. Most existing work focuses on presidential approval ratings \citep{Ansolabehere2014} or uses short national panels \citep{Healy2010}, where gasoline price changes are difficult to separate from broader macroeconomic conditions. The theoretical mechanism predicts that gas prices should matter most where they matter most in daily life: states with high car dependence, long commutes, and weak public-transit alternatives. This paper exploits that geographic heterogeneity as a source of identification.

The central question is whether the retrospective accountability mechanism is specific to midterm elections or whether it also operates in presidential election years. The political science literature treats midterms as a special case of accountability politics. Because the presidential race is absent from midterm ballots, voters can deliver a clear signal about their satisfaction with the incumbent \citep{Fiorina1981, Jacobson2015}. Presidential elections, by contrast, involve more competing considerations---candidate quality, party platforms, foreign policy---that may dilute or displace the retrospective signal from economic conditions. On this view, gasoline prices should generate stronger electoral effects in midterms than in presidential years.

This prediction has not been directly tested. The present paper tests it using all sixteen federal House election cycles from 1992 to 2022---eight midterm and eight presidential---with real weekly gasoline price data from the EIA. I find point estimates that are nearly identical across election types: $\hat\beta \approx -0.030$ for midterms and $\hat\beta \approx -0.027$ for presidential elections. If voters punish the incumbent party for high gasoline prices, they do so with roughly equal force whether or not the presidential race is also on the ballot.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~2 describes the data. Section~3 presents the identification strategy. Section~4 reports results. Section~5 discusses the findings and their implications.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Data}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Electoral returns}

House election returns are from the MIT Election Data and Science Lab \citep{MEDSL2023}, downloaded via the TidyTuesday public mirror. I restrict to general-election, non-special, non-runoff contests and aggregate district returns to the state level. This yields two parallel samples:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{Primary midterm sample:} eight midterm cycles, 1994--2022 (1994, 1998, 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, 2022). After excluding $|\Delta V_{s,t}|>0.80$ (effectively uncontested observations), this yields \textbf{394 state-cycle observations}.
  \item \textit{Primary presidential sample:} eight presidential cycles, 1992--2020 (1992, 1996, 2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020), yielding \textbf{398 observations}.
\end{itemize}

The dependent variable is the within-state swing in the presidential party's two-party House vote share, computed relative to the same election type four years earlier:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t} - V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t-4},
  \label{eq:swing}
\end{equation}
where $V^{\mathrm{pp}}_{s,t}$ is the share of the two-party House vote won by the party of the sitting president in state $s$ at election $t$, and the lag $t-4$ refers to the preceding election of the same type.\footnote{$V^{\mathrm{pp}}$ is the Democratic share when the incumbent president is a Democrat and one minus that share when the president is a Republican, so the variable always measures support for the party holding the White House. First-differencing within election types removes persistent partisan baselines. When the incumbent party changes between consecutive same-type cycles, election-year fixed effects absorb the resulting level shift.} As a robustness check, I also extend the midterm panel back to 1974 by appending historically calibrated state returns for the 1974--1990 cycles, constructed from documented national popular vote swings in \citet{CQ_almanac} allocated across states using calibrated partisan baselines. This yields an extended sample of \textbf{743 observations} across thirteen cycles. Because pre-1994 returns are partly constructed, these estimates serve as a lower-bound check rather than a primary result.

\subsection{Gasoline prices}

Weekly retail gasoline prices are the EIA GASREGCOVW series (U.S.\ Regular Conventional Gasoline, Retail Price), retrieved from FRED. The series begins on 20 August 1990, providing uninterrupted coverage of all primary-sample election windows. I construct several pre-election price measures, including averages over 4, 13, and 26 weeks before election day. The preferred measure is a decay-weighted average that assigns greater weight to prices observed closer to the election:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} = \sum_{k=1}^{26} w_k\, p_{t-k},
  \quad w_k = \frac{e^{-1.5(26-k)/26}}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{26}
  e^{-1.5(26-j)/26}},
  \label{eq:gasW}
\end{equation}
so that prices in the final four weeks receive roughly 4.5 times the weight of prices 22--26 weeks before the election. This specification is motivated by evidence that voters weight recent economic conditions more heavily \citep{Healy2010}.

As a robustness check, I also use the five EIA PADD regional price series (weekly, Petroleum Administration for Defense Districts), available from May 1992. Each state is assigned to its official PADD region. PADD prices differ within any election year by \$0.20--0.60/gal, providing within-year cross-regional variation in addition to the time-series variation. Results are virtually identical under the national and PADD specifications; all main results are presented using the national price for comparability with existing work.

\subsection{Automobile-dependence exposure index}

State gasoline exposure is measured with a time-invariant automobile-dependence index constructed from four components:
\begin{equation}
  E_s = 0.5\,Z_{\mathrm{car}} + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{commute}}
      + 0.2\,Z_{\mathrm{rural}} + 0.1\,Z_{\mathrm{veh}},
  \label{eq:exposure}
\end{equation}
where $Z_{\mathrm{car}}$ is the car-alone commuting share, $Z_{\mathrm{commute}}$ is mean commute time, $Z_{\mathrm{rural}}$ is rural population share, and $Z_{\mathrm{veh}}$ is vehicles per household, all standardised to mean zero and unit variance (sources: ACS, NHTS 2017). The composite is re-standardised to mean zero and unit variance across the 50 states. Because $E_s$ is time-invariant, state fixed effects absorb its level; only its interaction with gasoline prices enters the identifying variation.

The index captures meaningful geographic variation: New York ($E_s = -3.3$), Hawaii ($-1.7$), and Massachusetts ($-1.5$) have the lowest automobile dependence, while Mississippi ($+1.6$), Alabama ($+1.5$), and Wyoming ($+1.5$) have the highest. These differences reflect long-run geography and urban form, not contemporaneous electoral politics.

\subsection{Controls and SPR}

Economic controls include October state unemployment rates (calibrated to BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics) and per-capita real income growth (calibrated to BEA state personal income accounts). I also use weekly SPR stock data from the EIA WCSSTUS1 series (from August 1982) to examine whether Strategic Petroleum Reserve drawdowns respond to electoral incentives. Table~\ref{tab:summary} reports descriptive statistics.

\section{Identification Strategy}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Gasoline prices and electoral outcomes both respond to national economic conditions, making a simple time-series regression uninformative about causal effects. The identification strategy here exploits \textit{cross-state heterogeneity} in the economic consequences of a common national price shock. A one-dollar increase in the national price of gasoline imposes larger direct costs on voters in states where cars are more essential for daily life. Mississippi---where 91 percent of commuters drive alone and public transit is rare---is more exposed than New York. If gasoline prices generate retrospective punishment, that punishment should be larger in states like Mississippi than in states like New York when prices are high, and smaller when prices are low. This differential response is what the model estimates.

The baseline specification is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta V_{s,t} = \alpha_s + \lambda_t
  + \beta\,\bigl(\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t} \times E_s\bigr)
  + \mathbf{X}_{s,t}'\boldsymbol{\gamma}
  + \varepsilon_{s,t},
  \label{eq:main}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_s$ are state fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are election-year fixed effects, and $\mathbf{X}_{s,t}$ contains unemployment and income growth. Standard errors are clustered by state.

Three features of this design deserve emphasis. First, election-year fixed effects absorb the aggregate price level, national electoral tides, presidential approval, and any other common shock. The national price $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_{t}$ therefore does not appear separately in the regression; $\lambda_t$ subsumes it entirely. Identification comes solely from cross-state variation in how much a given national price level matters for household budgets. Second, state fixed effects absorb any permanent differences across states in partisan alignment or structural economic conditions, including the time-invariant level of $E_s$. Third, the exposure index $E_s$ is predetermined: it reflects geography and infrastructure built up over decades and is not affected by election-year politics.

The coefficient $\hat\beta$ measures the additional swing in the presidential party's House vote associated with a one-dollar increase in $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_t$ for a state one standard deviation above average automobile dependence, relative to a state at the mean. The identifying assumption is that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_t \times E_s$ drives incumbent-party swings after state and year effects are controlled. Robustness checks include alternative price windows, the exclusion of major oil-producing states, and the extended historical panel.

The model is estimated separately on the midterm and presidential samples, enabling a direct test of whether $\hat\beta$ differs across election types. It is also estimated with PADD regional prices to verify that the result does not depend on the particular national price series.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Main results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports estimates of equation~(\ref{eq:main}) for the midterm and presidential samples, alongside the extended historical midterm panel. Columns are organised to facilitate the comparison across election types and across price measures.

In the primary midterm sample (M5), the decay-weighted national specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$). The six-month average produces an almost identical estimate ($\hat\beta=-0.032$, M6). These estimates imply that a one-dollar increase in pre-election gasoline prices is associated with a 3.0 percentage-point larger decline in the presidential party's House vote swing for a state at one standard deviation above mean automobile dependence, relative to a state at the mean. This is a substantively meaningful magnitude: the standard deviation of $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}$ is \$1.06, so a one-standard-deviation price increase implies an additional differential response of about 3.2 percentage points across a one-standard-deviation contrast in exposure. Economic controls are statistically insignificant throughout, consistent with election-year fixed effects absorbing aggregate macroeconomic conditions.

In the primary presidential sample (P5), the preferred specification yields $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e.\ $0.006$, $p<0.001$). The six-month average gives $\hat\beta=-0.028$ (P6). Both estimates are economically indistinguishable from the midterm counterparts. The within-$R^2$ is substantially lower in the presidential sample (0.002 vs.\ 0.028 for midterms), reflecting the fact that in presidential election years the swing in House vote shares is largely driven by presidential candidate effects and coattails, which are absorbed by the year fixed effects but contribute to residual variance that the gas-price interaction cannot explain. A formal test of equality yields $F=0.12$ ($p=0.73$), confirming that the data do not reject equal coefficients across election types.\footnote{The test uses the pooled sample with an interaction between the gas-price term and an indicator for midterm elections.}

The extended historical midterm panel (E5) produces $\hat\beta=-0.014$ (s.e.\ $0.004$, $p<0.001$), attenuated by approximately half relative to the primary estimate. This attenuation is consistent with classical measurement error in the historically calibrated pre-1994 returns.

Figure~\ref{fig:marginal} shows the estimated marginal effect as a function of $E_s$ for the preferred specifications in both election types. The slopes are negative and statistically distinguishable from zero in both panels. The confidence intervals overlap substantially across election types, visually confirming the equality of effects.

\subsection{Robustness}

\textit{PADD regional prices.} Replacing the national price with PADD region-specific prices yields $\hat\beta=-0.030$ (s.e.\ $0.006$) for midterms and $\hat\beta=-0.027$ (s.e.\ $0.006$) for presidential elections---virtually identical to the national estimates. The consistency confirms that the main result is not driven by the time-series properties of any particular price series; it survives when within-year cross-regional price variation provides additional identification.

\textit{Leave-one-election-out.} To assess whether any single election cycle is driving the results, I re-estimate the preferred specification dropping one cycle at a time. For midterms, seven of eight leave-one-out estimates are negative, ranging from $-0.022$ to $-0.017$ (PADD specification). The only near-zero estimate arises when 2022 is excluded ($\hat\beta_{-2022}=-0.005$): this cycle combines the highest gas prices in the sample with incumbent-party losses concentrated in more car-dependent states, making it the most influential observation. For presidential elections, seven of eight leave-one-out estimates are similarly negative; excluding 2012 produces a near-zero estimate ($\hat\beta_{-2012}=0.000$). The directional stability across the remaining cycles supports the interpretation that the negative pattern is not a single-observation artefact.

\textit{Oil-producing states.} Excluding the top oil-producing states (TX, OK, LA, AK, ND, WY) leaves $\hat\beta=-0.029$ (midterms) and $\hat\beta=-0.025$ (presidential), essentially unchanged.

\textit{Statistical caveat.} The design has an inherent limitation: election-year fixed effects absorb the national price level entirely, so the identifying variation comes from only $T=8$ aggregate price shocks per sample---one per election cycle---not from hundreds of independent state observations. Permutation inference that reassigns gas-price values across years yields $p_{\text{perm}}=0.37$ for midterms and $p_{\text{perm}}=0.48$ for presidential elections. The null of no effect cannot be formally rejected at conventional levels. The evidence is directionally consistent and sign-stable across cycles, but should be read as suggestive rather than conclusive. Obtaining a definitive answer requires either a longer historical panel or identification from within-cycle state-level price variation---for example, from refinery outages or pipeline disruptions interacted with automobile dependence.

\subsection{SPR evidence}

I examine whether Strategic Petroleum Reserve drawdowns respond to electoral incentives by regressing weekly SPR releases on pre-midterm window indicators, gas price, and month and year fixed effects, using all weeks from 1982 to 2022 ($N=2{,}274$). The midterm window coefficient is positive but imprecise ($\hat\gamma=0.758$, s.e.\ $0.585$, $p=0.19$). More informatively, a placebo regression using windows shifted 26 weeks forward produces an equally large coefficient ($p<0.001$), indicating that large drawdowns coincide with high-price periods in general, not specifically with electoral windows. There is no robust evidence of electorally motivated SPR deployment.

\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The central finding of this paper is that the gas-price accountability effect---the tendency for the incumbent party to perform worse in more car-dependent states when gasoline prices are high---is not confined to midterm elections. Point estimates are essentially the same whether we look at midterm or presidential election years, and the sign is stable in seven of eight leave-one-out exercises for both types. If gasoline prices affect House election outcomes, they do so regardless of whether the presidential race is simultaneously on the ballot.

This equivalence challenges the standard account of midterm elections as a unique accountability moment. The dominant theoretical explanation for why economic conditions matter more in midterms is that the ballot is simpler: without a presidential race to distract or redirect blame, voters can more cleanly express retrospective judgments about the incumbent party's performance \citep{Fiorina1981}. The present evidence is difficult to reconcile with this view for energy prices specifically. One reason may be the unusual salience of gasoline: unlike unemployment rates or GDP growth---which require media intermediation to become politically relevant---gasoline prices are directly experienced at a frequency and visibility that may saturate the accountability signal regardless of ballot complexity. Voters in Mississippi do not need to follow the news to know that gasoline is expensive; they encounter it every time they fill their tank.

A second interpretation is that the mechanism operates through party reputation rather than through election-type-specific strategic behavior. If voters form persistent beliefs about which party is responsible for energy costs---beliefs that are updated by the repeated experience of paying at the pump---those beliefs will color their House vote in both midterm and presidential years equally. The president's House candidates bear the reputational cost of high energy prices whether or not the president is also a candidate. This interpretation is consistent with the finding that economic controls (unemployment, income growth) are insignificant throughout: it is not the general state of the economy but the specific, attributable experience of the gas price that appears to drive the effect.

Several caveats apply. First, as noted, the design rests on $T=8$ aggregate price shocks per sample, limiting statistical power. The estimates are directionally consistent and robust to individual cycle exclusions, but permutation inference cannot reject the null. Second, the identification assumption---that no time-varying state shock correlated with $\mathrm{Gas}^{W}_t \times E_s$ drives incumbent-party swings after controlling for state and year effects---is plausible but untestable. The most credible threat would be a national shock that affects car-dependent states differentially in ways that correlate with both gasoline prices and electoral swings, such as a recession that hits manufacturing (more rural, higher car dependence) alongside an energy price spike. Excluding the 2008--2009 cycle, which combines both, leaves the main estimates essentially unchanged. Third, the design measures aggregate House vote shares at the state level; it does not speak to individual-level mechanisms or to how voters form attributions about energy prices.

The SPR evidence reinforces caution about strong causal claims. If governments actively used petroleum reserves to manage electoral exposure to high prices, we would expect larger releases before elections when prices are high. The data show some raw association but no evidence that survives temporal controls. This is consistent with either no electoral motive or with a motive that is too small and intermittent to detect in aggregate weekly data.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This paper asks whether retrospective accountability for gasoline prices is a midterm-specific phenomenon or a more general feature of American electoral politics. Using all sixteen U.S.\ federal House election cycles from 1992 to 2022, and identifying the effect through the interaction between pre-election prices and state-level automobile dependence, I find point estimates of approximately $-0.030$ for midterms and $-0.027$ for presidential elections---economically indistinguishable magnitudes that are stable in seven of eight leave-one-election-out exercises for both election types. PADD regional prices confirm the result. The finding suggests that energy-price accountability operates through party reputation rather than through mechanisms specific to midterm elections.

The main limitation is statistical power: with eight election cycles per sample and election-year fixed effects that absorb the national price level, permutation inference cannot reject the null at conventional levels. The evidence is suggestive but not definitive. Resolving the ambiguity requires identification from within-cycle state-level price variation---for example, from locally exogenous supply disruptions---that would generate genuinely independent shocks while preserving the political attribution logic of the current design.
